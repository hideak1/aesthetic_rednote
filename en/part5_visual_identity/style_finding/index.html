    5.2 Finding Your Style¶

    "I want to create content that has my own style, but I have no idea what my style actually is."

    If that sounds like you, take it as a good sign — it means you are already thinking seriously about personal branding. Style does not fall from the sky one day. It grows, gradually, through a series of deliberate explorations.

    In this chapter, we walk through concrete methods for discovering yours.

    Start by Imitating¶

    Nobody invents a style from nothing. Every great creator began by imitating.

    Imitation is not copying

    Imitation is a learning process — you are studying why something looks good and why it works. Copying means reproducing someone else's work. Imitation means absorbing someone else's methods and then expressing them in your own way.

    Here is how to do it:

    1. Pick 3–5 accounts you admire. They do not have to be on Xiaohongshu — Instagram and Pinterest work too. Choose accounts that make you think, "I wish my page looked like that."
    2. Analyze their visual common ground. Ask yourself:
      • Does their color tone lean warm or cool? High saturation or low?
      • What compositions do they favor? Centered? Lots of whitespace? Edge-to-edge?
      • What style of typography do they use? Bold or thin? Serif or sans-serif?
      • What structural patterns do their covers share?
    3. Extract the patterns. You will often find that the accounts you love share certain visual traits. Those shared traits reveal your aesthetic preferences.

    Quick exercise

    Open your saved/bookmarked posts and screenshot 9 of your favorite covers. Arrange them in a 3x3 grid. Your aesthetic tendencies will be clearer than you expected.

    The Mood Board¶

    A mood board is a tool designers use constantly, and it is just as useful for Xiaohongshu creators. The idea is simple: collect visual elements you are drawn to in one place and look for patterns.

    Your mood board might include:

    • Colors you love (screenshots, swatches, or photos of real objects)
    • Font styles that appeal to you
    • Layout approaches you find attractive
    • Photos that feel like they have the "right vibe"
    • The emotions you want to convey (calm? energetic? premium? cute?)

    How to make one

    The easiest way: create an inspiration board on Pinterest and save every image that catches your eye. Once you have 30–50 images, step back and look at the whole board. Obvious visual preferences will emerge. You can also use Canva, or simply create a dedicated album in your phone's photo library.

    The point of a mood board is not to "finish" it — it is to turn a vague aesthetic feeling into a visible reference. When you are unsure what direction to take for your next post, flip through your mood board. The answer is usually already there.

    Style Keywords¶

    Now, try to define your desired style in three words.

    Why three? One is too vague, five is too complicated. Three words are enough to frame a clear direction while leaving room for flexibility.

    Some examples

    • Minimal x Gentle x Premium → low-saturation tones, generous whitespace, thin serif fonts
    • Playful x Colorful x Energetic → high-saturation palette, handwritten fonts, edge-to-edge layouts
    • Retro x Film x Literary → warm yellow tones, visible grain, vertical text
    • Clean x Intellectual x Minimalist → black-white-gray dominant, sans-serif fonts, lots of breathing room

    These three words become your "style anchors." Every time you make a design decision, ask yourself: does this choice align with my three keywords?

    Write them down

    Put your three style keywords in your phone's notes app, or stick them on your workspace wall. They are not a cage — they are a compass. When you are torn between options, they help you make decisions that stay true to your overall direction.

    Give Yourself Permission to Experiment¶

    This may be the most important thing in this entire chapter: do not try to lock in your style from day one.

    Finding your style is a process, not a moment. You will need to experiment, make mistakes, and publish some posts that you later feel were "not quite right." That is perfectly normal.

    Common mindset traps

    • "I need a perfect style from my very first post." → Unrealistic and unnecessary.
    • "Once I pick a style, I can never change it." → Style is alive; it grows with you.
    • "Other people's styles are better than mine." → Everyone's aesthetic soil is different; the flowers that bloom will naturally differ too.

    How to experiment well:

    1. Try things intentionally. Do not wander aimlessly — go in with a hypothesis each time. For example, "This week I will try a low-saturation look."
    2. Record how it feels. After publishing, ask yourself: Did I enjoy this style? Did the creative process feel natural? How did followers respond?
    3. Keep what works, let go of what does not. Over time, your style will "grow" out of these experiments.

    How long does it take to find your style?

    There is no standard answer. Some people find it after 10 posts; others are still exploring after 100. What matters is not speed — it is that your understanding of your own taste deepens with every attempt.

    Summary¶

    Finding your style does not require extraordinary talent — just a structured exploration process:

    1. Start by imitating — analyze accounts you love and extract their visual patterns
    2. Build a mood board — turn vague aesthetics into a visible reference
    3. Define style keywords — anchor your direction with three words
    4. Allow yourself to experiment — try intentionally, record your feelings, and grow

    Your style is already hidden inside your aesthetic preferences. All you need to do is bring it to light.
