Imagine your frame as a 3x3 grid. The four intersection points are where the eye naturally gravitates. Place your subject near one of these points and the image instantly feels balanced.

!!! tip "Quick win"
    Turn on your phone camera's grid overlay — your composition improves on the spot.

## Centered Symmetry

<figure style="margin: 1.5rem 0; text-align: center;">
  <img src="https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1600585154340-be6161a56a0c?w=400&h=500&fit=crop" alt="Centered symmetry example" loading="lazy" style="border-radius: 8px; max-width: 450px; width: 100%;">
  <figcaption style="font-size: 0.75rem; color: var(--text-muted); margin-top: 0.5rem;">Centered symmetry — ideal for architecture, interiors, and product shots</figcaption>
</figure>

Centered composition creates a feeling of **stability, elegance, and formality**. Works especially well for product shots, text-based covers, and architecture.

!!! warning "Watch out"
    Almost-centered looks sloppier than intentionally asymmetric. Take an extra moment to align your shot.

## Leading Lines

<figure style="margin: 1.5rem 0; text-align: center;">
  <img src="https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1477959858617-67f85cf4f1df?w=400&h=500&fit=crop" alt="Leading lines example" loading="lazy" style="border-radius: 8px; max-width: 450px; width: 100%;">
  <figcaption style="font-size: 0.75rem; color: var(--text-muted); margin-top: 0.5rem;">Roads and lines guide the eye, creating a sense of depth</figcaption>
</figure>

Leading lines are elements within the frame — roads, railings, light beams, table edges — that guide the viewer's gaze toward your subject. Lines should point toward your subject, and multiple converging lines amplify the effect.

!!! tip "Key point"
    Lines should point toward the subject, not out of the frame. Straight or curved both work.

## Negative Space

<figure style="margin: 1.5rem 0; text-align: center;">
  <img src="https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1507003211169-0a1dd7228f2d?w=400&h=500&fit=crop" alt="Negative space example" loading="lazy" style="border-radius: 8px; max-width: 450px; width: 100%;">
  <figcaption style="font-size: 0.75rem; color: var(--text-muted); margin-top: 0.5rem;">Generous negative space makes the subject stand out with room to breathe</figcaption>
</figure>

**Less is more.** Give your image room to breathe and your subject naturally stands out. Whitespace also creates natural zones for text and signals sophistication.

!!! warning "Avoid this"
    An overloaded cover looks like a classified ad. Stick to **one core message** per cover.

## Contrast

Contrast is the most effective way to create visual hierarchy. Without it, the image is flat; with it, the viewer instantly knows what matters.

| Contrast type | How to do it | Effect |
|--------------|-------------|--------|
| Size | Large headline + small subtitle | Establishes information hierarchy |
| Focus | Sharp subject + blurred background | Highlights the subject |
| Brightness | Bright subject on a dark background | Creates a focal point |
| Color | Complementary colors (e.g., blue + orange) | Grabs attention |
| Density | Dense cluster of elements next to open space | Directs the gaze |

!!! tip "Contrast in practice"
    A red lipstick on a pure white background — color contrast makes it pop. Small text with the shade name next to it — size contrast keeps the hierarchy clear.

## Takeaways

1. **Rule of thirds** is a universal starting point — when in doubt, use it
2. **Centered symmetry** suits formal, premium-feeling content
3. **Leading lines** give the image a sense of direction
4. **Negative space** is your secret weapon for sophistication
5. **Contrast** separates the main subject from everything else
