# 2.4 Tools & Practice

You've got the theory and the rules — the final step is putting them to work in your actual content. This chapter recommends practical color tools and walks you through a complete start-to-finish workflow.

## Recommended Tools

### Xingtu (醒图)

Xingtu is one of the most popular photo editing apps among Xiaohongshu creators, packed with filters and color adjustment features.

!!! tip "Xingtu Color Tips"
    - The HSL panel lets you adjust saturation and brightness for individual hues, giving you precise control over your image's tone
    - Built-in filters can unify your entire image's color palette in one tap
    - The "Templates" feature offers ready-made color schemes — great for beginners

**Best for:** Photo color grading, filter-based tone unification, quick edits

---

### Canva

Canva is an online design platform whose biggest advantage is that **templates come with built-in color schemes**.

- Search "Xiaohongshu" to find templates pre-sized for the platform
- Every template has a preset palette — click any color block to see the full scheme
- Supports custom color replacement — find a template with colors you like, swap in your own content

!!! info "Canva's Hidden Feature"
    Upload any image you love, and Canva will automatically extract its dominant colors into a palette. This is a fantastic way to discover color inspiration.

**Best for:** Cover design, palette reference from templates, quick layouts

---

### Coolors

[Coolors](https://coolors.co) is a professional online palette generator.

- Press the spacebar to randomly generate a 5-color palette
- Lock colors you like and regenerate only the others
- Supports extracting palettes from uploaded images
- Includes a colorblind accessibility checker

**Best for:** Finding palette inspiration, exploring new combinations, professional color work

---

### Adobe Color

[Adobe Color](https://color.adobe.com) is Adobe's powerful color tool.

- Interactive color wheel — select rules like "complementary," "analogous," or "triadic" to auto-generate palettes
- The "Explore" page showcases trending color themes
- Supports palette extraction from images
- Checks color contrast against accessibility standards

!!! example "When to Use Adobe Color"
    When you've settled on a primary color but aren't sure what to pair it with, input it into Adobe Color and select "complementary" or "analogous." The tool will generate a harmonious palette for you automatically.

**Best for:** Systematic wheel-based palettes, contrast checking, professional design

---

### Butter Camera (黄油相机)

Butter Camera's strength is **typography and text overlays**, with solid color features as well.

- Extensive font template library, each with recommended color pairings
- Stickers and filters help unify the overall image tone
- Supports custom text colors with direct hex code input

**Best for:** Text layout, title overlays on covers, text color consistency

---

## Step-by-Step Workflow

Tools in hand, here's a complete workflow for color matching. Follow these 5 steps every time you design a cover:

### Step 1: Define Your Style

Ask yourself: what mood should this post convey?

| Target Mood | Recommended Palette |
|-------------|-------------------|
| Sophisticated, serene | Morandi tones |
| Vibrant, eye-catching | High-saturation contrast |
| Soft, comforting | Cream tones |
| Professional, premium | Dark & Gold |

### Step 2: Choose Your Dominant Color

From the corresponding palette, pick one dominant color (60%). This will be your background or base color.

### Step 3: Pick a Secondary Color

From the same palette, select a secondary color (30%) for supporting elements — color blocks, decorations, subheadings.

### Step 4: Set Your Accent

Choose a color with some contrast to your dominant shade as the accent (10%). Use it for titles, buttons, and key highlights.

### Step 5: Unify with a Filter

Finally, apply a cohesive filter in Xingtu or Butter Camera to bring all your colors into the same tonal family.

!!! warning "A Common Mistake"
    Many creators nail the first four steps but skip the fifth. Without a unifying filter, even perfectly chosen hex codes can clash when photos and text don't share the same tonal quality.
