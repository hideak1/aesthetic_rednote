# 1.3 Layout & Information Hierarchy

The previous two chapters covered composition and typography. This chapter ties them together — how to organize and arrange all the information on a single cover. The core question is simple: **can a viewer glance at your cover and know what it's about within one second?**

## Information Hierarchy

Not every element on a cover is created equal. They need a clear pecking order, and the tools for establishing it are straightforward: **size, weight, and color**.

### The Four-Layer Structure

| Layer | Content | Visual treatment | Example |
|-------|---------|-----------------|---------|
| Layer 1: Headline | The cover's core hook | Largest, boldest, most prominent | "3-Step Breakfast Hack" |
| Layer 2: Subheading | Supporting context or qualifier | Medium size, slightly muted color | "For busy mornings · 10 min total" |
| Layer 3: Body | Key details | Smaller font, stays out of the spotlight | Ingredient list, step numbers |
| Layer 4: Details | Decorative or auxiliary info | Smallest, lightest | Logo, date, watermark |

!!! tip "The arm's-length test"
    After finishing a cover, hold your phone at arm's length. If your eye lands on the headline first and the subheading second, the hierarchy is working. If everything blurs into a single mass of text, it's time to adjust.

!!! warning "When everything shouts, nothing is heard"
    If every element is big, bold, and bright, nothing stands out — which is the same as having no hierarchy at all. Be willing to let secondary information stay quiet so the headline can be loud.

## Visual Flow

Visual flow describes the path a viewer's eye takes across the image. Understanding these patterns lets you place the most important information exactly where the eye lands first.

### Z-Pattern

Best for **landscape (horizontal)** covers. The eye starts at the top left, sweeps to the top right, cuts diagonally to the bottom left, then sweeps to the bottom right — tracing a Z.

| Position | What to place there |
|----------|-------------------|
| Top left | Brand mark or attention-grabbing element |
| Top right | Headline or primary keyword |
| Bottom left | Supporting info or hook text |
| Bottom right | Call to action or logo |

### F-Pattern

Best for **portrait (vertical)** covers — and Xiaohongshu's most common cover ratio is 3:4. The eye reads across the top, scans downward, then reads across again roughly in the middle-left area, forming an F shape.

!!! info "What the F-pattern tells us"
    On vertical covers, the **top two lines are prime real estate**. Place your headline there for maximum impact. The middle area is ideal for the hero image or product, and the bottom is best for secondary information.

### Core Principles of Flow Design

1. **Follow natural reading habits** — don't force the eye to jump around
2. **Place key information at the start of the flow** (top-left or top-center)
3. **Place secondary information at the end of the flow** (bottom-right or bottom)

## Information Density

Information density is how much a single cover tries to communicate. This is one of the most common areas where beginners go wrong.

!!! warning "The number-one trap: trying to say too much"
    "This product has great color, smooth texture, lasts all day, costs next to nothing, AND comes with free samples…" You want every selling point on the cover, but the viewer remembers none of them.

**Golden rule: one cover, one core message.**

Think about how you browse Xiaohongshu yourself — your thumb flies through the feed and each cover gets less than a second of attention. In that sliver of time, how many ideas can you absorb? Exactly one.

!!! tip "The one-sentence check"
    Summarize your cover's message in a single sentence. If that sentence runs longer than about ten words, you're packing in too much — time to cut.

| Density | Effect | Best for |
|---------|--------|----------|
| Low (1 core message) | High visual impact, strong click-through | Product recs, outfit shares, single-product reviews |
| Medium (2-3 points) | Balanced, but requires solid layout skills | Tutorials, guides, comparison reviews |
| High (4+ points) | Easily cluttered; demands advanced design chops | Roundups, list-style posts (not recommended for beginners) |

## Layout by Post Type

Different content types call for different layout strategies. Here are the three most common Xiaohongshu post types and how to approach each.

### Tutorials

The key to a tutorial cover is **clarity of steps**. The viewer should immediately understand "this is a tutorial, and here's what I'll learn."

- Headline at the top, stating what the tutorial teaches
- Middle area can feature 2-4 small images showing steps or before-and-after
- Bottom can carry a nudge like "Follow along" or "Save for later"
- Numbered labels (1, 2, 3) help establish sequence

!!! example "Tutorial layout in action"
    Top, in large text: "5-Min Commute Makeup." Middle, split into two columns: bare face on the left, finished look on the right. Bottom, in small text: "All drugstore products." Simple, clear, and clickable.

### Product Recommendations

The key to a product-rec cover is **making the product the star**. Everything else plays a supporting role.

- The product should fill at least 60% of the frame
- Keep the background clean — solid color or a simple setting
- Text should be minimal: brand name + one-line hook
- Use close-ups and detail shots to build desire

### Guides & Roundups

Guide covers are naturally information-heavy, so the key is **visible structure**. The viewer should think "lots of info, but well-organized."

- Use numbered lists to create a sense of order
- Dividers or colored blocks can separate different sections
- The headline should clearly state the scope — e.g., "Tokyo 5-Day Complete Guide"
- Icons (transport, lodging, food) can help convey categories at a glance

!!! tip "Less is more — even for guides"
    Save the detailed itinerary for the post body. The cover only needs to tell the viewer "here's what this guide covers" and "it's worth tapping into." Don't try to compress the entire guide onto a single image.

## Takeaways

Information hierarchy and layout are the final piece of the cover-design puzzle. Keep these core principles close:

1. **Build a clear hierarchy** — headline > subheading > body > details
2. **Follow visual flow** — place the most important info where the eye lands first
3. **Control density** — one cover, one core message
4. **Match the content type** — tutorials need clarity, product recs need a hero shot, guides need structure

Put all three chapters together and you have a complete cover-design methodology. From here, it's practice, observation, and iteration.
