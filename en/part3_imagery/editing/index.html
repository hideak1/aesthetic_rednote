    3.2 Editing & Filters — Unify Your Style, Elevate Your Quality¶

    Selecting great images is only the first step. Through post-processing and color grading, you can take your visuals to the next level. But editing is a double-edged sword — done well, it enhances; overdone, it detracts.

    The Core Principle of Color Grading: Consistency¶

    The single most important rule of photo editing is: consistency.

    Every image within a single post must maintain a unified color tone. If image 1 is warm yellow, image 2 is cool blue, image 3 is highly saturated, and image 4 is black and white — that mismatch makes the entire post feel chaotic and significantly hurts the reading experience.

    How to Achieve Consistency

    • Apply the same filter or the same set of grading parameters to all images in a post
    • If shooting conditions varied, first unify white balance and exposure before applying filters
    • Save your frequently used presets to build a signature style

    Consistent color grading does not just make individual posts look better — it gives your entire profile a cohesive visual identity. When users visit your profile and see a unified aesthetic, they are far more likely to follow.

    Popular Filter Styles¶

    Here are the most popular filter aesthetics on Xiaohongshu, each suited to different content types:

    Japanese Style¶

    Characteristics: High brightness, low saturation, warm tones, soft overall feel

    Best for: Daily life, food, literary fashion, cafe reviews

    Grading Direction

    Slightly increase exposure, reduce contrast, lower saturation, push color temperature warm, shift highlights toward yellow, lift shadows. The goal is a "soft and warm" feeling throughout.

    Korean Style¶

    Characteristics: Cool tones, clean and transparent feel, low saturation, crisp and polished

    Best for: Skincare, minimalist fashion, minimal home decor, atmospheric content

    Grading Direction

    Push color temperature cool, lower saturation, moderate contrast, shift highlights toward blue-white, keep skin tones natural. Aim for a "cool, elevated" aesthetic.

    Vintage Style¶

    Characteristics: Warm yellow tones, low contrast, slight film grain

    Best for: Travel, street photography, film-inspired daily life, vintage fashion

    Grading Direction

    Push color temperature warm, shift shadows toward yellow-green, shift highlights toward orange, lower contrast, add a touch of grain. Emulate the look of analog film.

    Muted Luxury Style¶

    Characteristics: Low saturation, high perceived quality, restrained color palette, overall gray tonality

    Best for: Premium products, minimalist aesthetics, architecture photography, elevated fashion

    Grading Direction

    Significantly lower saturation, keep contrast moderate to slightly high, push the overall tone toward gray, retain a hint of the dominant color. Pursue the restrained beauty of "less is more."

    Recommended Editing Tools¶

    Different tools suit different needs and skill levels:

    Tool Strengths Best For Platform
    Xingtu Feature-rich, abundant templates, Chinese-friendly interface Beginners to intermediate iOS / Android
    VSCO Exceptionally high-quality filters, refined color aesthetics Users who prioritize quality iOS / Android
    Lightroom Professional-grade grading, precise parameter control Intermediate to professional iOS / Android / Desktop
    Snapseed Made by Google, excellent local adjustment tools Users who need detailed retouching iOS / Android

    Beginner Recommendation

    If you are just starting out, begin with Xingtu. Its filter templates can be applied with one tap, and you can manually fine-tune the parameters. Once you are comfortable with grading logic, move on to VSCO or Lightroom.

    Editing Pitfalls: Mistakes to Avoid¶

    The biggest risk in editing is trying too hard. Here are the most common ways things go wrong:

    Over-Smoothing Skin¶

    The Plastic Face Problem

    Smoothing skin until the nose disappears and the texture looks like plastic — this kind of editing is not only unattractive but also erodes trust. Retain some natural skin texture for a result that looks both real and beautiful.

    Inconsistent Filters¶

    Using different styles across images in the same post is a cardinal sin. Even if each image looks fine individually, clashing styles destroy the overall visual coherence when viewed together.

    Brightness Too High or Too Low¶

    Overexposure washes out the image and loses detail; underexposure makes content hard to see. After editing, try lowering your screen brightness and checking again — many people keep their phone brightness permanently high, which causes their edited images to appear too dark on other devices.

    Over-Sharpening¶

    Moderate sharpening can enhance clarity, but overdoing it creates unnatural white edges and noise. The image ends up looking "harsh" and "artificial."

    The Highest Level of Editing

    When someone thinks "this photo looks amazing" but cannot pinpoint what was edited — that is the gold standard. Great post-processing is invisible. It elevates quality without leaving a trace.

    Chapter Summary¶

    • Rule number one of color grading: Consistency, consistency, consistency
    • Find the filter style that fits you: Japanese, Korean, vintage, and muted luxury each have their strengths
    • Tools — quality over quantity: Mastering one tool is better than dabbling in ten
    • Exercise restraint: Over-editing is worse than not editing at all

    In the next chapter, we will discuss multi-image arrangement — once your images are selected and edited, the order in which you present them matters more than you might think.
