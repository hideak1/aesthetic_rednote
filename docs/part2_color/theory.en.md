# 2.1 Color Theory

On Xiaohongshu, color is often the very first impression a user gets from your post. Before you start picking hex codes, spend a few minutes understanding the fundamentals — this knowledge will serve you far better than memorizing any single palette.

## The Color Wheel

The color wheel is the foundational tool for understanding how colors relate to each other.

!!! info "Primary Colors"
    At the core of the color wheel are three primary colors: **Red**, **Yellow**, and **Blue**. Every other color is derived from mixing these three.

**Secondary colors** are created by mixing two primaries in equal amounts:

| Mixture | Secondary Color |
|---------|----------------|
| Red + Yellow | Orange |
| Yellow + Blue | Green |
| Red + Blue | Purple |

**Complementary colors** sit directly opposite each other on the wheel — red & green, blue & orange, yellow & purple. Placed together, they create strong visual contrast. Used skillfully, this creates a striking, high-end look. Used carelessly, it looks garish.

!!! tip "A Rule to Remember"
    The closer two colors are on the wheel, the more harmonious they feel. The farther apart, the stronger the contrast. Before picking colors, ask yourself: do I want harmony or impact?

## The HSB Model

Rather than memorizing specific hex codes (like #8EA4B5), understanding three dimensions of color will help you much more.

**H — Hue**

Hue is what we colloquially mean by "color" — red, orange, yellow, green, blue, purple. It corresponds to a position on the color wheel, measured from 0° to 360°.

**S — Saturation**

Saturation controls how vivid or muted a color appears. Higher saturation means more vivid and intense; lower saturation means greyer and softer.

| Saturation | Effect | Good For |
|-----------|--------|----------|
| High (80-100%) | Vibrant, energetic, eye-catching | Beauty, food, fashion-forward |
| Medium (40-70%) | Comfortable, natural | OOTD, travel, home decor |
| Low (10-30%) | Sophisticated, quiet, grey-toned | Morandi, wabi-sabi |

**B — Brightness**

Brightness controls how light or dark a color is. High brightness leans toward white; low brightness leans toward black.

!!! example "In Practice"
    Take blue: high saturation + high brightness = Klein Blue (bold and energetic), while low saturation + medium brightness = dusty blue (soft and serene). Once you understand HSB, you can derive an entire palette from a single starting color.

## Color Temperature

Colors have a perceived "temperature" that directly shapes the mood of your image.

**Warm tones: Red, Orange, Yellow**

- Evoke warmth, passion, energy, and approachability
- Great for food, outfit, home decor, and lifestyle content
- Psychologically close the distance between you and the viewer

**Cool tones: Blue, Green, Purple**

- Evoke calm, rationality, freshness, and sophistication
- Great for tech, business, travel, and minimalist content
- Create a sense of space and detachment

!!! info "Xiaohongshu Trend"
    Mixing warm and cool tones has been hugely popular on the platform recently — think warm-toned imagery with cool-toned text, or a cool filter with warm accent touches. This blend creates images that feel both inviting and elevated.

## Color Psychology

Every color unconsciously triggers specific emotions and associations. Understanding these effects lets you use color to communicate before a single word is read.

| Color | Psychological Association | Content Fit |
|-------|--------------------------|-------------|
| **Red** | Passion, energy, urgency, festivity | Sales, beauty, holidays |
| **Blue** | Calm, trust, professionalism, breadth | Tech, business, travel |
| **Green** | Nature, health, freshness, relaxation | Fitness, food, outdoors |
| **Pink** | Softness, sweetness, romance | Beauty, OOTD, dating |
| **Yellow** | Joy, liveliness, attention-grabbing | Food, parenting, study tips |
| **Black** | Luxury, mystery, power | Tech, luxury goods, men's content |
| **White** | Clean, minimal, breathing room | Minimalist, Japanese style, medical beauty |

!!! warning "A Caveat"
    Color psychology is not absolute. The same hue at different saturation, brightness, and in different combinations can convey entirely different moods. A bright red screams passion; a dark burgundy might feel somber. Always consider context.

## Takeaway

Understand the color wheel, master the three HSB dimensions, sense temperature differences, and learn color psychology — with these fundamentals, you're no longer picking colors by gut feeling.
