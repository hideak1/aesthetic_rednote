# 3.1 Image Selection Principles — Good Images Speak for Themselves

On Xiaohongshu, a single great image outweighs a thousand words of copy. Selecting images is not as simple as "picking something pretty" — there are clear, actionable standards. Master these five core principles and your image quality will improve dramatically.

---

## Subject Prominence: Make It Instantly Clear

The subject is the soul of your image. In a proper Xiaohongshu photo, the subject should occupy at least 60% of the frame.

Users spend no more than 1-2 seconds on each image while scrolling. If the subject is not prominent enough, they will swipe past before even registering what you are sharing.

!!! tip "Practical Advice"
    Get closer to your subject when shooting, or crop aggressively during editing. A tight composition is always better than a subject that is too small.

**Common mistakes:**

- In outfit posts, the person occupies only one-third of the frame, surrounded by cluttered background
- Product shots taken from too far away, missing important details
- Food photos where the plate sits in a tiny corner

**Quick test:** After taking a photo, shrink it to thumbnail size on your phone. Can you still clearly identify the subject? If not, re-crop or re-shoot.

## Clean Backgrounds: Supporting, Not Competing

The background's job is to complement the subject, not compete with it for attention.

!!! info "Three Types of Great Backgrounds"
    - **Solid colors:** White walls, plain tabletops, solid fabric — clean and versatile
    - **Blurred backgrounds:** Use portrait mode or a wide aperture to naturally isolate the subject
    - **Simple scenes:** A tidy desk, a clean street, an organized space

Cluttered backgrounds are the most common beginner mistake. Objects piled on a desk, strangers in the background, posters on the wall — all of these pull attention away from what matters.

!!! warning "Save Yourself Time"
    Spending 30 seconds tidying the background before shooting saves far more time than fixing it in post-production. Build the habit of "tidy first, shoot second."

## Lighting: Natural Light Is the Best Filter

Lighting directly determines the quality and feel of your image. The same subject can look completely different under different lighting conditions.

**Three commonly used lighting directions:**

| Lighting Type | Effect | Best For |
|---------------|--------|----------|
| Front light | Bright, clear image with accurate colors | Product shots, full-body outfit photos |
| Side light | Creates depth and dimension with highlights and shadows | Portraits, food, texture showcase |
| Backlight | Strong atmosphere, creates a halo or glow effect | Lifestyle vlogs, mood-driven scenes |

!!! tip "Golden Shooting Hours"
    Natural light is softest between 9-11 AM and 3-5 PM. Midday sun is too harsh and creates heavy shadows. Overcast days act as a natural softbox — perfect for shooting.

When shooting indoors, position yourself near a window to take advantage of natural light. If light is insufficient, use a white card as a reflector — this produces much better results than a flash.

## Sharpness: Blurry Images Are a Deal-Breaker

This seems like the most basic point, yet it is overlooked with surprising frequency.

Blurry images give the impression of carelessness and lack of effort. This is especially critical for cover images — a blurry cover is essentially telling users to keep scrolling.

**Common causes of blur:**

- Camera shake: not holding the phone steady
- Mis-focus: the focal point lands on the background instead of the subject
- Low light: the camera compensates with a slower shutter speed
- Over-cropping: zooming in too much degrades image quality

!!! tip "Tips for Staying Sharp"
    - Hold your phone with both hands, elbows tucked against your body
    - Tap the subject on screen to manually set focus
    - In low light, use a tripod or rest your phone on a stable surface
    - Take burst shots and pick the sharpest one

## Emotional Storytelling: Great Images Have a Narrative

Once the technical boxes are checked, the final dimension is emotion.

Truly compelling images are not just sharp and attractive — they create a sense of immersion. The viewer sees a photo and thinks, "I want to visit that place," "I want to own that thing," or "I want to live that kind of life."

!!! example "Techniques for Emotional Impact"
    - **Include human elements:** A hand holding a coffee cup, a person's silhouette, an open book on a table — traces of human presence add warmth
    - **Capture motion:** The moment coffee is poured, a page being turned, hair caught by the wind — movement feels more alive than static poses
    - **Use light and color:** Warm afternoon sun, raindrops on a window, golden sunset tones — light and shadow are emotions in themselves

Do not just aim for "pretty." Aim for "feeling." Technique is the foundation, but emotion is what elevates an image from adequate to outstanding.

## Chapter Summary

The five core principles of image selection, ranked by importance:

1. **Sharpness** — The baseline; a blurry image disqualifies everything else
2. **Subject prominence** — Make the content instantly recognizable
3. **Clean background** — Eliminate distractions
4. **Lighting** — The key to elevating quality
5. **Emotional storytelling** — The difference between good and great
