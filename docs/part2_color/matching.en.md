# 2.3 Matching Rules

You've picked your palette — now it's time to distribute those colors across your canvas. Good color matching isn't about piling attractive colors together randomly; it's about proportion, hierarchy, and intention. This chapter covers the most practical matching rules and the pitfalls you must avoid.

## The 60-30-10 Rule

The most classic color proportion rule in design — simple yet remarkably effective.

!!! info "The 60-30-10 Rule"
    - **60% Dominant color:** The large-area base, usually the background. Sets the overall tone.
    - **30% Secondary color:** Used for supporting elements like color blocks, decorations, and subheadings. Adds depth.
    - **10% Accent color:** The most eye-catching color, reserved for titles, buttons, and key information. The finishing touch.

**Applied examples:**

| Element | Ratio | Morandi Scheme | Dark & Gold Scheme |
|---------|-------|---------------|-------------------|
| Background | 60% | Milk Tea #C8B49A | Rich Black #1A1A1A |
| Blocks/Decor | 30% | Dusty Blue #8EA4B5 | Charcoal #333333 |
| Titles/Focus | 10% | Rose Beige #C4A882 | Gold #C9A96E |

!!! tip "A Universal Trick"
    Not sure if your proportions are right? Squint at your finished cover. If you can clearly sense "one large area of base color + some supporting color + a pop of brightness," your ratios are on track.

## Monochromatic Matching

Monochromatic matching is the safest, most foolproof approach.

**What is monochromatic?** A set of colors derived from a single hue by varying its saturation and brightness. Light blue, medium blue, and dark blue form a monochromatic set.

**Advantages:**

- Naturally harmonious — nearly impossible to look bad
- Creates a unified, clean, polished appearance
- Perfect for maintaining a consistent grid aesthetic

**How to do it:**

1. Pick a base hue (e.g., blue)
2. Use the lightest shade as your background
3. Use the mid-tone for supporting elements
4. Use the darkest shade for text or titles

!!! example "Monochromatic Example"
    Blue family: pale grey-blue (background) → dusty blue (color blocks) → deep navy (titles). Three shades from the same hue, automatically harmonious.

**Watch out for:**

- Ensure at least 3 levels of light-dark variation; otherwise the design will look flat
- Add small amounts of neutral colors (white, grey, beige) as transitions

## Complementary Matching

Complementary colors sit far apart on the color wheel — typically directly opposite. Pairing them creates bold visual tension.

**Classic complementary combos:**

| Combo | Visual Effect | Use Case |
|-------|--------------|----------|
| Red + Green | Festive, energetic | Needs a buffer color |
| Blue + Orange | Modern, sporty | Tech, fitness |
| Purple + Yellow | Luxurious, edgy | Streetwear, art |

!!! warning "The Key to Complementary Pairing"
    Done well, complementary colors look like intentional, high-end contrast. Done poorly, they're an eyesore. The secrets:

    1. **Unequal area:** Let one color dominate; use the other as an accent
    2. **Add buffers:** Separate the two with white, grey, or beige
    3. **Desaturate one:** If both colors are vivid, dial back the saturation on one of them

## Common Pitfalls

These are the most frequent color disasters on Xiaohongshu. Steer clear.

### Pitfall 1: Large Areas of Neon

Neon green, neon pink, neon yellow — covering large areas with neon colors makes designs look cheap and straining on the eyes.

!!! warning "Neon Isn't Banned"
    Neon can work beautifully as a small-area element in titles or icons. Just never use it as a background.

### Pitfall 2: Red and Green Without Transition

Red and green are complementary, and slamming them together in large blocks without a buffer creates an unrefined, dated look. If you must use both, separate them with white, beige, or grey.

### Pitfall 3: More Than 4 Colors Without Hierarchy

A cover using 5 or 6 colors in roughly equal proportions looks chaotic and unfocused.

!!! tip "Color Count Guideline"
    Stick to 3-4 colors maximum per design, with a clear primary-secondary hierarchy. If you must use more, let 1-2 colors dominate.

### Pitfall 4: Low Contrast Between Text and Background

Light grey text on a white background, dark blue text on a black background — when text and background are too similar, readability tanks.

**Quick test:** Turn your phone brightness to minimum. If you can't read the text, your contrast is too low.

| Rule | Core Point | Difficulty |
|------|-----------|-----------|
| 60-30-10 | Dominant 60%, secondary 30%, accent 10% | Easy |
| Monochromatic | Same hue, varying brightness/saturation | Easy |
| Complementary | Opposite colors, mind the ratio and buffers | Medium |
| Pitfall Checklist | Avoid neon, raw contrast, too many colors, low contrast | — |
